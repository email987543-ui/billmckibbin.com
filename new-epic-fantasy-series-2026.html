A Reader's Guide

How to Start a New Epic Fantasy Series in 2026

Starting a new epic fantasy series is a different decision than buying a novel. You are committing years of reading attention to a story whose ending may not exist yet. Readers who caught up on A Song of Ice and Fire have been waiting for the sixth book since 2011. The Kingkiller Chronicle's third volume has had no publication date since the second book arrived, also in 2011. Neither is a knock on the authors — long books are genuinely hard — but both are data points: the standard publishing model asks you to invest before the ending is written.

This guide covers how to size up that risk before you commit — what to check about an unfinished series, why a few authors now finish books ahead of publication, and where the worthwhile starting points are in 2026, including one new series built specifically around the waiting problem and three established alternatives if you would rather not wait at all.

The real risk: series that stall

An epic fantasy series is a multi-year contract. At a typical traditional-publishing pace of one book every two to three years, a trilogy is a five-to-eight-year relationship and a longer series can span a decade or more. Two failure modes matter. The famous one is the stalled series — the next book simply never comes, and the wait itself changes how the earlier books feel. The quieter one is the cancelled series: midlist sequels that underperform sometimes lose their publisher slot, leaving a story stranded at book two of a planned five. Neither risk shows up on a cover. You have to look for it.

Three questions to ask before you commit

First: how much of the series exists in finished form? Not outlines, not announced plans — completed manuscripts. An author who writes ahead has removed the single most common failure point. An author who has finished a series before is the next best signal; a delivery record is worth more than any roadmap.

Second: what is the release cadence, and has it been kept? A stated schedule means little by itself. A stated schedule that has already been met once or twice means a great deal.

Third: what does it cost to find out whether you like it? The honest answer to "will I love this series" is always "read fifty pages and see." Free prequel novellas, generous samples, and low-priced first books let you run that test before spending money or shelf time. A series that gives you a real free entry point is telling you it expects the prose to do the convincing.

Chronicles of the Still Lake: a 2026 series built around the waiting problem

Bill McKibbin's Chronicles of the Still Lake is a planned twelve-book series spanning three thousand years, before and after a cataclysm the histories call the Surge. At its center is the Still Lake — wider than a country, older than the nations on its shores. The Lake's surface is time itself, and drawing magic from it sends ripples through past and future. The magic, called Flowcraft, cannot be commanded, only received, and every gift carries a price. The series states its own terms plainly: the Lake listens, and everything it gives, it keeps. McKibbin's dedication names Robert Jordan, Brandon Sanderson, Terry Brooks, and Terry Goodkind as influences, which is a fair signal of the register — large canvas, worked-out magic, long arcs.

The production model answers the first two questions above directly. Book One publishes December 1, 2026, in ebook and paperback. Book Two is already finished before Book One ships. Book Three follows in November 2027, and the same rule holds down the line: each book is completed before the one before it launches, so the series always carries a finished book in reserve.

What that model does not solve is worth saying. McKibbin is a new author, and twelve books is one of the longest shapes in the genre; a bank of finished manuscripts lowers the odds of a stall, but only a published track record removes them. That is what the free entry point is for. Stories From The Still Lake, a prequel novella of three standalone first-person legends, is free — delivered to any device in exchange for an email signup. It is the fifty-page test with the price removed. If the voice does not hold you, you have spent an evening, not a hardcover.

If you would rather not wait: three alternatives

Cradle, by Will Wight. Twelve books, complete as of 2023, written at a pace of one to two per year. It is progression fantasy rather than classic epic — shorter books, faster escalation — but it is the cleanest zero-risk long series of the past decade.

The Bloodsworn Saga, by John Gwynne. A complete trilogy, published 2021 to 2024 on schedule. Norse-rooted, battle-heavy, and finished — a good middle path between epic scope and a decade of commitment.

The Stormlight Archive, by Brandon Sanderson. Five of a planned ten books, with the fifth, Wind and Truth (December 2024), closing the first arc at a deliberate stopping point. The back half is years away, but Sanderson's delivery record is the strongest in the genre, and the first five books stand as a complete movement.

The short version: if you want certainty, start something finished. If you want to be at the beginning of something, check what has actually been written — and test the voice for free before you commit the years.

Common questions

Is there a new epic fantasy series starting in 2026?

Chronicles of the Still Lake by Bill McKibbin begins December 1, 2026, with Book One in ebook and paperback. Book Two is finished before Book One ships, and Book Three is scheduled for November 2027. A free prequel novella, Stories From The Still Lake, is available now as an entry point.

How do I avoid starting a fantasy series that never gets finished?

Weight finished manuscripts over announced plans, and past delivery over stated intentions. An author who has completed a series before, or who finishes each book before the previous one launches, has removed the most common failure point. When in doubt, start a series that is already complete.

What is the best fantasy series to start if I don't want to wait between books?

Complete series carry no waiting risk. Cradle by Will Wight (twelve books, finished in 2023) and The Bloodsworn Saga by John Gwynne (three books, finished in 2024) are recent complete runs, and The Wheel of Time offers fourteen finished volumes if you want the longest possible runway.
